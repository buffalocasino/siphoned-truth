# Day 79: The Sanctions Paradox — New Restrictions, Open Door, and a Strait That's Still Closed

## The Official Position

On one channel: Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent is on the phone with G7 finance ministers, urging unified enforcement of sanctions designed to strangle financing to Iran's war machine. The message is maximum pressure, sustained, relentless. Allies should hold the line.

On another channel: Trump is telling reporters he is "considering lifting" sanctions on Chinese companies that buy Iranian oil — a diplomatic concession offered in the wake of his Xi summit, framed as something Beijing should take as a gesture of goodwill.

On a third channel, not publicly acknowledged: the Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed, Iraq's exports have collapsed, and Iran has established a toll authority that is extracting fees on whatever still moves through.

## What the Data Actually Shows

Let's start with the timeline, because the contradiction here is not subtle.

**May 11 — Reuters**: The US announces new sanctions on 3 people and 9 companies, including Hong Kong-based entities, for their roles in Iran oil shipments. This is not a softening. This is the enforcement track — going after the evasion infrastructure, the shippers, the intermediaries, the Chinese firms that keep Iranian crude flowing into the market.

**May 15-16 — Trump**: "Considering lifting" sanctions on Chinese companies that buy Iranian oil. This is presented as a Xi summit concession. The implicit message: we're prepared to relieve pressure on exactly the firms the May 11 sanctions were designed to squeeze.

These two moves are four days apart. They cannot both be coherent policy. You don't tighten the vise on a Tuesday and signal you're opening it by the weekend and call that a negotiating strategy.

**The Physical Reality**

While the administration runs its contradictory signal operation, the Strait of Hormuz is not cooperating with the narrative.

Iraq's oil minister confirmed Iraq exported only 10 million barrels through the Strait in April. The previous baseline was 93 million barrels per month. That is an 89% drop. It did not recover in May.

Iran announced the formation of what it calls the "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" — an operational body to manage the Strait, including the collection of tolls. First Vice President Aref stated Iran "will no longer allow 'enemy' military equipment to transit the Strait of Hormuz." The word "enemy" covers the US, Israel, and any other flagged vessel Iran chooses to designate.

The Strait moves roughly 20% of global oil. It is not moving it normally.

**The Beijing Problem**

Trump said Xi agreed Iran should reopen the Strait. Beijing has not confirmed this. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi stated reopening depends on a "permanent and comprehensive ceasefire" — which does not exist.

This is not a diplomatic rejection. It is something more precise: a condition that cannot currently be met, offered publicly, by the foreign minister of the country Trump is trying to bring into his diplomatic concession framework. Wang Yi is not saying no. He is saying: come back when the war is actually over.

Trump appears to have announced a concession that the recipient has not publicly accepted, tied to a condition that does not yet exist.

**The G7 Coordination Farce**

Bessent's push for unified G7 enforcement of sanctions is the other half of the paradox. The US is asking allies to hold the line on sanctions enforcement while simultaneously floating relief to the primary target of those sanctions. Allies are being asked to enforce a regime the US president is signaling he may waive. 

This is not how alliance diplomacy works. You can have a sanctions regime or you can have a diplomatic concession framework. Running both simultaneously doesn't produce leverage — it produces confusion, and the confusion primarily benefits the parties you're trying to pressure, who can now play different US officials against each other.

**The Market Signal**

There is one other reading of the "considering lifting" announcement that bears examination: it is a market operation.

Oil markets are absorbing the physical reality of an 89% drop in Iraqi exports through a Strait that is functionally closed. Brent has not returned to pre-conflict baselines. The administration wants prices lower — lower energy costs are a domestic political asset, and the inflation signal from the IMF's 4.4% inflation baseline is not a comfortable one.

Floating a sanctions relief story — even a conditional, unconfirmed one — is a known technique for cooling market anxiety. It doesn't require the relief to actually happen. It only requires the story to circulate.

Beijing has not endorsed the story. The lifting is "considered," not offered. The market effect, if any, depends on whether traders believe it. The conditions for believing it — a ceasefire that doesn't exist — are not present.

## Siphoned Analysis

The administration is running sanctions theater. New restrictions on Tuesday, open-door signals by the weekend, G7 enforcement requests, a Chinese toll authority operating in the Strait, and 89% drops in Iraqi exports that nobody in the US chain of command is publicly acknowledging.

This is not a negotiating strategy. It is a communications strategy — designed to produce headlines, not outcomes. The outcomes are visible in the April export numbers: 10 million barrels instead of 93 million. A Strait that Iran controls, not the US. A toll authority that is collecting fees from whatever still moves through. A Chinese foreign minister who has publicly stated the condition for reopening and knows that condition is not met.

You cannot simultaneously impose new sanctions on a country's oil supply chain and signal you're preparing to lift those same sanctions, and call it coherent policy. You cannot ask G7 allies to enforce a regime your own president is floating relief from. You cannot declare the Strait "secure enough" while it remains effectively under Iranian operational control.

The real story is not the "considering lifting" headline. The real story is the Strait, the export numbers, and the toll authority. Those are not signals. Those are facts.
